Expectations of an Architect

Fundamentals of Software Architecture — Richards & Ford, 2020

Why this matters to you: You don't need to be an architect to think like one. Every senior developer makes micro-architecture decisions daily — which pattern to use, how to structure a module boundary, when to push back on a design. These eight expectations define the mental model you're building toward.

The Eight Expectations

Richards & Ford define the role of a software architect through eight core expectations. These aren't job requirements — they're the behaviors that distinguish architectural thinking from pure implementation thinking.

01 Make architecture decisions

Define the architecture decisions and design principles that guide technology choices across the team — not make every decision, but set the guardrails others work within.

→ As a developer: when you propose "we should use event sourcing here," you're doing this at small scale.

02 Continually analyze the architecture

Regularly assess the current architecture and technology environment, identify weaknesses, and recommend improvements — even when nothing is broken yet.

→ As a developer: noticing that a service is accumulating too many responsibilities is this skill in action.

03 Keep current with latest trends

Track industry trends and emerging technologies — not to chase hype, but to know what options exist when trade-offs need to be made.

→ As a developer: this is what separates someone who picks the right tool from someone who only knows the tool they learned first.

04 Ensure compliance with decisions

Verify that development teams are following the agreed-upon architectural decisions and design principles — through code review, pairing, and tooling, not just documentation.

→ As a developer: understanding why a decision exists makes you more likely to follow it (and push back correctly when it shouldn't apply).

05 Diverse exposure and experience

Have breadth across technologies, frameworks, platforms, and environments — not depth in one. An architect who only knows one stack has a single lens for every problem.

→ As a developer: this is why senior devs intentionally pick up unfamiliar problems rather than staying in their comfort zone.

06 Have business domain knowledge

Understand the business domain well enough to align architectural decisions with business goals. Technical excellence that contradicts business needs is a liability, not an asset.

→ As a developer: knowing why a feature matters lets you make better trade-offs when implementing it.

07 Possess interpersonal skills

Teamwork, facilitation, and negotiation. Architecture happens through people. The best technical decision rejected by the team is worse than a good-enough decision everyone follows.

→ As a developer: this is why "being right" isn't enough — you need to bring people along.

08 Understand and navigate politics

Almost every significant architectural decision will be challenged. Architects must navigate organizational dynamics to get good decisions implemented — not as compromise, but as skill.

→ As a developer: watch how senior engineers propose changes. The technical argument is only part of the pitch.

Recall Check

Close your notes. Read each question and pick your answer. You'll get immediate feedback. The goal is to retrieve — not recognize.

1. An architect's job is to make every technical decision in the system. True or false?

Correct. Expectation #1 is about setting decision guardrails and design principles, not micromanaging every choice.

2. Which expectation most directly explains why a senior developer should deliberately work with unfamiliar technologies?

Correct. Expectation #5 is specifically about breadth — having experience across many technologies so you can reason about options rather than defaulting to what you already know.

3. A technically superior architecture that the team refuses to adopt is:

Correct. Expectation #7 (interpersonal skills) exists precisely because the best decision unimplemented is useless. Architecture is a social act.

4. Name the two expectations that are explicitly about people and organizational dynamics (not technology).

Correct. Expectations #7 and #8 are explicitly human/organizational. The book devotes significant space to these because they surprise most technically-minded people who assume architecture is purely technical.

5. Why does an architect need business domain knowledge?

Correct. Expectation #6 — technical decisions divorced from business context create architectures that are correct in a vacuum but harmful in practice.
